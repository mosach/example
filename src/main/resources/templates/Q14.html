GDPR Checklist

GDPR Compliance Checklist

GDPR IQ generates the documents you need to comply with the GDPR. But there are several other steps your organization needs to take to fully comply.

  1. Notice. Your organization needs to provide a Privacy Notice to the public.  Many organizations use email or a public-facing website to distribute the notice.  You can generate a Privacy Notice through GDPR IQ.
  2. Tracking Consent. Your organization needs to track who it has gathered consent from. This means your organization must (1) record when and how it received consent, (2) keep records of the forms used to gather consent, and (3) review who has given you consent and withdrawn it, so you can stop handling information if you no longer have consent to handle it. You can generate a notice/consent form through GDPR IQ.
  3. Privacy Requests. Your organization needs to develop a process to respond to privacy requests, including:
  1. a process to provide people copies of their personal information, and
  2. a process for deleting personal information when asked.

You can generate forms to track privacy requests through GDPR IQ.

  1. Disposing of Information. Your organization needs to implement a process for securely disposing of personal information when your organization no longer needs that information.  
  2. Automated Processing. If your organization uses automated systems to process personal information, your organization needs to provide a method for people to intervene in decisions made by automated systems.    
  3. Data Processing Agreements. Your organization needs to add a Data Processing Agreement into contracts where your organization deals with third parties regarding personal information from the Europe. You can generate Data Processing Agreements through GDPR IQ.
  4. Security. Your organization needs to implement security policies and controls. You can generate security policies and controls through GDPR IQ.
  5. Special Categories of Data. If your organization handles the following personal information, consider talking to an attorney for advice about how to handle that information.
  1. Information from a child below the age of 16
  2. Information about a criminal offense
  3. Information about a person's race
  4. Information about a person's ethnic origin
  5. Information about a person's political opinions
  6. Information about a person's religious or philosophical beliefs
  7. Information about a person's trade union membership
  8. Information about a person's genetics
  9. Information about a person's biometrics
  10. Information about a person's health
  11. Information about a person's sex life or sexual orientation
  1. Labor Laws. If your organization employs people in the European Union, consider talking to an attorney in Europe for more information about labor and union laws.  
  2. Training. Your organization needs to train its employees on the organization’s GDPR Policies and Procedures.
  3. Representative in the EU. Your organization needs to retain a representative in the EU. This representative needs to be located in a country where the people whose data you have live and reside.
